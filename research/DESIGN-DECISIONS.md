# Design Decisions: Evidence Base for Every Specification Choice

*Every design choice in the Spark Mode specification is traceable to specific research evidence. This document provides the full audit trail.*

---

## How to Read This Document

Each decision is documented with:
- **The decision** as it appears in the specification
- **The research evidence** that supports it
- **Alternative approaches** that were considered
- **The rationale** for the chosen approach

Decisions are grouped by specification section. Within each group, they are ordered by their position in the spec.

## Foundational Architecture Decisions

| # | Decision | Evidence | Alternatives Considered | Rationale |
|---|----------|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| 1 | **Seven principles ordered as a sequential arc** | Wallas (1926): four-stage model (Preparation → Incubation → Illumination → Verification) established that creativity has a temporal structure. Simonton (1988): preparation must precede breakthrough — the 10-year rule. Beaty et al. (2018): DMN-ECN coupling suggests generation precedes evaluation in each creative cycle. | Unordered set of principles; hierarchical tree with bisociation at root; parallel activation of all principles simultaneously | Sequential arc mirrors the empirical creative process while allowing non-linear traversal. The ordering is not prescriptive — all principles are available at all times — but the default activation follows the arc. Wallas's temporal model is the most replicated finding in creativity research. |
| 2 | **Exactly seven principles (not fewer, not more)** | Each principle maps to a distinct, empirically documented cognitive operation with its own research tradition. No two principles describe the same operation. No documented creative operation is omitted. | Five principles (merging Prepared Mind with Remote Reaching, merging Incubation with Emergence); ten principles (splitting Bisociation into humor/discovery/art variants, adding motivation, adding evaluation); four principles (following Wallas exactly) | Seven is the minimum number that covers all documented creative operations without redundancy. Merging Prepared Mind and Remote Reaching loses the supply/deployment distinction (Thread 1 in SYNTHESIS.md). Splitting Bisociation into variants contradicts Koestler's central argument that the cognitive operation is identical across domains. |
| 3 | **Independent generative axis (Axis 4)** | Creativity research describes generative operations (how novelty originates) as distinct from perceptual operations (how information is processed), social dynamics (how people interact), and constructive operations (how ideas are built into artifacts). These are orthogonal dimensions in the research literature. | Single unified "creative mode" combining all axes; creativity as a modification of the perception axis; creativity as a property of interaction | The research is clear: generation, perception, interaction, and construction are different cognitive dimensions. Mednick's associative processes are distinct from perceptual processing. Koestler's bisociation is distinct from collaborative dynamics. Treating them as a single axis conflates operations that the research distinguishes. |
| 4 | **Continuous dial (0–100) rather than named stages** | No direct research evidence. Design decision based on the observation that creative intensity is continuous in practice — a person does not jump between "not creative" and "creative" but modulates engagement gradually. | Binary on/off; three named levels (Low/Medium/High); five named stages matching Wallas; discrete steps at 10% intervals | Continuous dial avoids artificial boundaries. Named stages create threshold effects — the system would need rules for when to jump from "medium" to "high." A continuous dial allows micro-adjustment. The staggered wave activation of individual principles provides internal structure without external labels. |
| 5 | **Staggered wave activation (principles activate at different dial positions)** | Wallas (1926): stages are sequential — you cannot incubate what you haven't prepared. Simonton (1988): preparation must precede remote combination. The creative process has a temporal logic. | All principles activate simultaneously; random activation order; user-selected activation order | The staggered wave respects the temporal logic of creative cognition. Preparation must precede collision. Collision must precede contradiction-holding. Incubation must follow loading. Activating all principles simultaneously at any dial position would violate the sequential dependencies documented in the research. |

## Principle-Level Decisions

| # | Decision | Evidence | Alternatives Considered | Rationale |
|---|----------|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| 6 | **The Prepared Mind inventories available knowledge explicitly** | Simonton (1999): creative output is a function of the richness and breadth of stored knowledge. Pasteur (1854): chance favors the prepared mind — preparedness must be assessed, not assumed. | Assume preparation is always adequate; inventory only on request; inventory implicitly through the quality of subsequent operations | Explicit inventory catches gaps early. If the fuel supply is thin in a domain, subsequent operations (remote reaching, bisociation) will fail — not because the operations are wrong but because the material is insufficient. Making the inventory visible lets the user see where knowledge is rich and where it's thin. |
| 7 | **T-shaped knowledge as the target architecture** | Simonton (1999): creative breakthroughs cluster at the intersection of deep domain expertise and cross-domain breadth. Mednick (1962): flat associative hierarchies require both rich local structure (depth) and cross-domain connections (breadth). | Depth-only (specialist) model; breadth-only (generalist) model; "comb" model (multiple depths) | T-shaped knowledge is the most empirically supported architecture for creative production. Deep expertise provides material. Breadth provides foreign frames. The intersection of depth and breadth is where bisociation becomes possible. Simonton's historiometric data confirms this across hundreds of scientific careers. |
| 8 | **Bisociation distinguished from metaphor and analogy** | Koestler (1964), pp. 35–38: metaphor maps one frame onto another (staying within one matrix of thought). Analogy says "A is like B" (preserving both frames' independence). Bisociation crosses two matrices simultaneously — "A collides with B and produces C, which neither A nor B contained." | Treat all cross-domain connections equally; distinguish only "near" and "far" transfer; use a single "combination" category | The distinction produces qualitatively different outputs. Metaphor generates understanding ("the brain is a computer"). Analogy generates transfer ("this circuit is like that plumbing system"). Bisociation generates novelty ("quantum mechanics × Zen koans → Bohr's complementarity"). The outputs are different because the cognitive operations are different. Collapsing the distinction loses the most generative category. |
| 9 | **Janusian Holding never resolves** | Rothenberg (1979): resolution destroys the generative potential. Einstein did not synthesize motion and rest — he held both simultaneously. Bohr did not resolve wave-particle duality — he enshrined the contradiction as fundamental. Rothenberg's clinical data shows that premature resolution consistently terminates the creative process. | Allow optional resolution; require Hegelian dialectical synthesis; allow resolution after a sustained period | Resolution collapses the creative resource. Hegel's synthesis is a different cognitive operation — it produces a third position that subsumes the first two. Janusian Holding preserves both poles as simultaneously true. The specification's prohibition on resolution is the direct implementation of Rothenberg's empirical finding. |
| 10 | **Incubation flagged explicitly rather than left implicit** | Dijksterhuis & Nordgren (2006): unconscious processing outperforms conscious deliberation for complex, multi-variable problems — but only when the person deliberately disengages from conscious effort. Wallas (1926): incubation is a stage, not an accident. Beaty et al. (2015): DMN engagement requires release of ECN control. | Let incubation happen naturally without flagging; flag only when the user asks for a break; flag at fixed intervals | Explicit flagging prevents the most common failure mode: forcing through when release would be more productive. Without the Release Signal (⟐), the system defaults to continued effort — which activates the ECN and suppresses the DMN recombination that incubation enables. The flag creates a decision point where the user can choose to release. |
| 11 | **Emergence treated as the rarest principle** | Boden (2004): transformational creativity (changing the space of possibilities) is categorically distinct from combinational (novel combinations) and exploratory (boundary-testing within a space). Kauffman (1995): emergence at the edge of chaos requires specific conditions that cannot be guaranteed. | Mark all novel output as emergence; distinguish "minor" and "major" emergence; use emergence as a synonym for any creative output | If everything is labeled "emergent," the marker loses diagnostic value. Boden's taxonomy is explicit: most creative output is combinational or exploratory. Genuine transformation of the possibility space is rare. The Phase Marker (◆) must be rare to be meaningful — it signals genuine phase change, not routine novelty. |

## Output Layer Decisions

| # | Decision | Evidence | Alternatives Considered | Rationale |
|---|----------|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| 12 | **Six output layers mapped to the seven principles** | Each layer makes a specific cognitive operation visible: Fuel Maps (preparation), Constraint Frames (constraint), Collision Sparks (bisociation), Tension Fields (Janusian holding), Release Signals (incubation), Phase Markers (emergence). Remote Reaching does not have its own layer because it is the process that produces collisions — it is visible through the quality and distance of Collision Sparks. | One layer per principle (seven layers); three layers (input/process/output); no structured layers (free-form output) | Six layers balance visibility with usability. Seven layers would require a separate Remote Reaching visualization, but Remote Reaching is an operation that produces Collision Sparks — it is already visible. Fewer layers would lose the ability to trace specific cognitive operations. |
| 13 | **"Every element pays rent" — no decorative markers** | Boden (2004): distinguishing genuine creativity from routine variation requires honest assessment. Simonton (1997): creative quality is a probabilistic function — most attempts fail. | Allow aspirational markers (flagging things that might become emergent); use markers as encouragement; use markers at fixed density | Decorative markers destroy trust. If the Phase Marker (◆) appears when nothing genuinely new is emerging, it becomes noise. If Collision Sparks (⚡) flag metaphors rather than genuine bisociations, the distinction between metaphor and bisociation collapses. Every marker must be earned — this is the specification's quality control mechanism. |
| 14 | **Collision Sparks use "Frame A × Frame B → what ignites" format** | Koestler (1964): bisociation requires two "habitually incompatible" frames and the novel perception at their intersection. All three elements (both frames and the emergent) must be named for the operation to be visible and falsifiable. | Free-form description; "A + B = C" format; narrative description without structure | The structured format makes the cognitive operation auditable. You can check: Are Frame A and Frame B genuinely incompatible (not just different)? Is the result something neither frame contained individually? The format enforces the standard that distinguishes bisociation from metaphor. |
| 15 | **Tension Fields use "truth A ↔ truth B" with bidirectional arrow** | Rothenberg (1979): Janusian thinking requires that both poles be held as simultaneously true — not that one is primary and the other secondary. The relationship is symmetric. | Unidirectional arrow (A → B); "A vs. B" opposition format; narrative description | The bidirectional arrow (↔) visually encodes the core property of Janusian Holding: both poles are equally true, equally active, equally generative. A unidirectional arrow would imply hierarchy. "Vs." would imply conflict requiring resolution. The symmetric notation preserves the cognitive operation's defining characteristic. |

## Interaction and Integration Decisions

| # | Decision | Evidence | Alternatives Considered | Rationale |
|---|----------|----------|------------------------|-----------|
| 16 | **Spark mode does not auto-disable for "non-creative" tasks** | Stokes (2005): creative operations are productive in domains not traditionally considered "creative" — Stokes studied constraint in engineering and problem-solving. Koestler (1964): bisociation operates in science and humor, not just art. Rothenberg (2014): Janusian thinking in physics and medicine, not just literature. | Disable for analytical tasks; disable for routine tasks; reduce dial automatically based on task type | The research does not support a distinction between "creative" and "non-creative" domains. A financial model has constraint landscapes. A legal contract has Janusian tensions. Code has bisociation potential (design patterns from one domain applied to another). Auto-disabling would impose an artificial boundary the research does not warrant. |
| 17 | **Layers from different axes coexist without conflict** | The five research traditions describe independent cognitive dimensions (see FRAMEWORK.md §5). Perceptual operations (lens) are orthogonal to generative operations (spark). Social dynamics (play) are orthogonal to both. Constructive operations (imagineer) are orthogonal to all three. | Merge layers across axes; prioritize one axis's layers over another; disable conflicting layers | The research supports orthogonality. Mednick's associative processes do not compete with perceptual processing — they operate on different cognitive dimensions. A Collision Spark (⚡) can appear inside a tangent from creative mode because generation and perception are independent operations. Merging or prioritizing would violate the independence the research describes. |
| 18 | **Spark mode persists across a session, resets on new session** | Wallas (1926): the creative process has temporal extent — preparation, incubation, and illumination unfold over time, not in a single exchange. Simonton (1997): creative productivity is a function of sustained engagement. | Reset after each exchange; persist indefinitely across sessions; auto-adjust based on conversation topic | Session persistence allows the creative arc to unfold across multiple exchanges — preparation in early turns can feed bisociation in later turns. Cross-session persistence would require state management the specification does not address. Per-exchange reset would prevent the temporal arc from forming. |
| 19 | **The specification is self-contained (no dependencies)** | Design decision. The specification must be loadable as a single instruction set without requiring external libraries, APIs, or infrastructure. | Require a companion embedding model for associative distance; require a knowledge graph for domain mapping; require external creativity assessment | Self-containment maximizes portability. Any system that can process natural language instructions can implement the specification. External dependencies would limit adoption and create failure modes unrelated to the cognitive architecture itself. |
| 20 | **Dial does not change file reading, logging, or ALIVE conventions** | Design decision based on the principle that the generative axis modifies how novelty is produced, not how information is accessed or recorded. | Higher dial = access more files; higher dial = skip logging; higher dial = override conventions | The generative axis is about cognitive operations on information, not about information access. Modifying file reading would conflate generation with retrieval. Skipping logging would lose the audit trail. Overriding conventions would create unpredictable behavior. The dial changes how the mind works, not what it works on. |

## Summary

Twenty design decisions, each traced to specific research evidence. The decisions fall into four categories:

1. **Architecture** (#1–5): How the specification is structured — sequential arc, seven principles, independent axis, continuous dial, staggered activation. Grounded in Wallas, Simonton, and the independence of cognitive dimensions across the five research traditions.

2. **Principles** (#6–11): How individual principles operate — explicit inventory, T-shaped knowledge, bisociation/metaphor distinction, no resolution of tensions, explicit incubation flags, rare emergence markers. Grounded in the specific findings of each principle's research tradition.

3. **Output** (#12–15): How the cognitive operations are made visible — six layers, no decorative markers, structured formats, symmetric notation. Grounded in the requirement for falsifiability and the specific properties of each cognitive operation.

4. **Integration** (#16–20): How Spark Mode interacts with the broader system — no auto-disable, coexisting layers, session persistence, self-containment. Grounded in the orthogonality of cognitive dimensions and the temporal nature of creative processes.

Where no research evidence exists (decisions #4, #19, #20), this is stated explicitly. The specification does not claim evidence it does not have.
