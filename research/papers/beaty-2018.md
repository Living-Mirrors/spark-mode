# Robust Prediction of Individual Creative Ability from Brain Functional Connectivity

**Authors:** Roger E. Beaty, Mathias Benedek, Paul J. Silvia, Daniel L. Schacter
**Published:** *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS)*, 115(5), 1087–1092, 2018
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1713532115
**Field:** Cognitive Neuroscience, Creativity Research, Connectomics

---

## Abstract

This study demonstrates that individual creative ability can be predicted from functional connectivity patterns measured by resting-state fMRI. Using connectome-based predictive modeling (CPM), the authors identified a brain network — characterized by strong functional connectivity between the Default Mode Network (DMN), Executive Control Network (ECN), and Salience Network (SN) — that reliably predicted individual differences in divergent thinking ability. The model was trained on one dataset (N = 163) and validated on three independent samples (total N = 327), demonstrating robust cross-sample generalization. This is the strongest evidence to date that creative ability has a measurable neural signature.

## Key Findings

- **Creative ability is predictable from brain connectivity.** A connectome-based predictive model trained on resting-state fMRI data predicted individual divergent thinking scores in three independent samples. This is not a post-hoc correlation — it is a genuine prediction of unseen participants' creative ability from their brain scans alone.
- **Three-network model.** The predictive connectivity involves three networks: (a) the Default Mode Network (spontaneous generation, memory recombination); (b) the Executive Control Network (directed evaluation, goal maintenance); (c) the Salience Network (switching between DMN and ECN, detecting relevant information). Creative individuals show stronger functional connections among all three.
- **DMN-ECN coupling is key.** The single strongest predictor is the functional connectivity between the DMN and ECN — precisely the coupling that would allow fluid alternation between spontaneous generation and critical evaluation. Highly creative individuals don't just generate more freely OR evaluate more critically — they do both, and switch between them more fluidly.
- **Cross-sample generalization.** The model trained on one sample (N = 163, University of North Carolina) predicted creative ability in three independent samples: (a) Austrian participants (N = 79); (b) Chinese participants (N = 135); (c) an additional US sample (N = 113). This cross-cultural generalization strengthens the claim that the neural signature is fundamental rather than culturally specific.

## Methodology

Connectome-based predictive modeling (CPM) applied to resting-state fMRI data. Participants completed resting-state scans (eyes open, fixation cross, 5–10 minutes) and behavioral creativity measures (alternative uses task for divergent thinking). Brain connectivity was computed as Pearson correlations between time series of 268 brain regions (Shen atlas). CPM used leave-one-out cross-validation to identify connectivity features that predicted creative ability, then tested on held-out samples. Training sample: N = 163 (University of North Carolina). Validation samples: N = 79 (University of Graz, Austria), N = 135 (Southwest University, China), N = 113 (Harvard University).

> "Creative thinking may require the coactivation of brain networks that typically work in opposition — the default network (associated with spontaneous thought) and executive network (associated with cognitive control)."

## Limitations

The creativity measure (alternative uses task) captures one aspect of creative ability (divergent thinking) but not others (convergent creativity, artistic quality, scientific insight). The resting-state paradigm measures trait-level connectivity but not the dynamic state changes that may occur during actual creative production. The effect sizes, while statistically significant, are moderate — brain connectivity explains a portion of the variance in creative ability but far from all of it. Environmental factors, training, and motivation are not captured by brain scans.
