# The Art of Thought

**Author:** Graham Wallas (1858–1932), London School of Economics and Political Science
**Published:** Harcourt, Brace and Company, New York, 1926. Reprinted by Solis Press, 2014. ISBN (reprint): 978-0-9562209-5-5
**DOI/Link:** https://archive.org/details/artofthought0000wall
**Field:** Psychology, Education, Political Science

---

## Abstract

*The Art of Thought* proposes the first systematic stage model of creative thinking, drawing on introspective accounts from Helmholtz, Poincare, and other scientists and mathematicians. Wallas identifies four stages: (1) Preparation — conscious, deliberate engagement with the problem; (2) Incubation — the problem is set aside, no conscious effort directed at it; (3) Illumination — the solution appears, often suddenly and unbidden; (4) Verification — the solution is consciously evaluated and refined. The model has remained the most widely cited framework in creativity research for a century, with each stage subsequently receiving independent empirical validation.

## Key Findings

- **The four-stage model.** Creative thought proceeds through four identifiable stages:
  1. **Preparation:** Conscious, deliberate work on the problem — gathering information, defining constraints, attempting solutions.
  2. **Incubation:** The problem is set aside. No conscious effort. Wallas explicitly argues that "the thinker's mind is not merely 'at rest' during incubation but is actively — though unconsciously — processing the problem."
  3. **Illumination:** The solution appears, often suddenly and in contexts unrelated to the problem. Poincare's Fuchsian functions while stepping onto a bus. Helmholtz's solutions while walking in the morning.
  4. **Verification:** The solution is consciously evaluated, tested, and refined. Not all illuminations survive verification.
- **Incubation is not rest.** Wallas emphasizes that incubation is an active unconscious process, not mere relaxation. The mind continues to work on the problem without conscious direction. This anticipates the DMN research by 87 years.
- **Intimation.** Wallas also describes "intimation" — a growing feeling that illumination is approaching, before the solution itself arrives. This phenomenological detail has been less studied but is consistent with the gradual activation patterns observed in DMN research.
- **The stages are not rigid.** Wallas notes that the stages may overlap, recur, and iterate. A complex creative project may involve multiple preparation-incubation-illumination cycles within the larger arc.

## Methodology

Theoretical synthesis based on introspective accounts from scientists (Helmholtz, Poincare, W.S. Jevons) and Wallas's own observations. No controlled experiments. The book is a scholarly argument drawing on published autobiographical accounts, psychological theory (William James, associationism), and educational philosophy. The four-stage model is proposed as a descriptive framework, not a tested hypothesis.

## Relevance to Spark Mode

**Principle:** #6 — Incubation (and structural framework for the entire seven-principle arc)
**Specific connection:** Wallas's four-stage model provides the temporal backbone for Spark Mode's seven-principle arc. Preparation maps to Principles #1–3. Illumination maps to Principles #4–5. Incubation maps to Principle #6. Verification (evaluation) is handled by the ECN in the DMN-ECN model. Emergence (Principle #7) extends beyond Wallas's model — it addresses what Wallas called illumination at a higher resolution, distinguishing routine insight from genuine phase change.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The first temporal model of creative thought. Every subsequent creativity researcher has worked within or against Wallas's framework. The four-stage model established that creativity has a temporal structure — it is not a momentary flash but a process that unfolds over time, with identifiable stages that serve different cognitive functions.

## Key Quotes

> "Helmholtz... has given us, in the words which he used at a dinner given in his honour, a description of the way in which his most important new thoughts came to him. He said that after previous investigation of the problem 'in all directions... happy ideas come unexpectedly, without effort, like an inspiration.'"

> "If we accept the four-stage analysis, it follows that in the daily stream of thought these four different stages constantly overlap each other as we explore different problems."

> "The incubation stage... is not a mere absence of conscious thought about the problem. It is a period during which a series of unconscious and involuntary mental events may take place."

## Limitations

*The Art of Thought* predates modern cognitive psychology and neuroscience. The four-stage model is based on introspective accounts, which are unreliable indicators of cognitive process. The stages are descriptive rather than mechanistic — Wallas identifies what happens but cannot explain how or why. Subsequent research has refined and complicated each stage: preparation involves specific cognitive operations (Mednick, Stokes); incubation involves specific neural systems (Jung, Beaty); illumination involves specific cognitive acts (Koestler, Rothenberg). The model remains valuable as a framework but has been superseded in mechanistic detail.
