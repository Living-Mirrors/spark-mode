# The End of Certainty: Time, Chaos, and the New Laws of Nature

**Author(s):** Ilya Prigogine (Universite Libre de Bruxelles; University of Texas at Austin). Nobel Prize in Chemistry, 1977.
**Published:** Free Press, 1997
**ISBN:** 0-684-83705-6
**Field:** Thermodynamics / Complexity Science

---

## Abstract

Ilya Prigogine, who received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for his work on dissipative structures, argues that the deterministic, time-reversible worldview of classical physics is fundamentally incomplete. The key concept is the dissipative structure: a system maintained far from thermodynamic equilibrium by a continuous throughflow of energy and matter, which spontaneously organizes into complex, coherent patterns. Examples range from Benard convection cells (hexagonal patterns emerging in heated fluid) to chemical oscillations (the Belousov-Zhabotinsky reaction) to living organisms. In far-from-equilibrium conditions, systems reach bifurcation points where small fluctuations are amplified into macroscopic organizational changes — the system "chooses" between multiple possible new states. This choice is irreversible and unpredictable from the prior state. Prigogine argues this is not noise or error but the fundamental mechanism by which nature generates novelty. Time's arrow is real, creativity is built into the fabric of physics, and the future is genuinely open.

## Key Findings

- **Dissipative structures: order from fluctuation.** Systems maintained far from equilibrium by energy throughflow spontaneously organize into coherent structures. These structures are "dissipative" because they exist only as long as the energy flow continues — they are maintained by the process of dissipation, not despite it. The Benard cell is the canonical example: heat a thin layer of fluid from below, and above a critical temperature gradient, the fluid spontaneously organizes into hexagonal convection cells — a macroscopic pattern emerging from molecular chaos.
- **Bifurcation points.** As a far-from-equilibrium system is driven further from equilibrium (e.g., the temperature gradient increases), it reaches critical thresholds — bifurcation points — where the current organizational state becomes unstable. At the bifurcation, the system transitions to a new organizational state. Which new state is selected depends on small fluctuations that are amplified at the critical point. The outcome is deterministic in its dynamics but unpredictable in its specific trajectory.
- **Irreversibility is fundamental.** Classical physics treats time as reversible — the equations work equally well forward and backward. Prigogine argues that irreversibility is not an illusion or an approximation but a fundamental feature of nature at the macroscopic level. The second law of thermodynamics, which gives time its arrow, is not a consequence of our ignorance but a reflection of the constructive role of irreversibility in nature.
- **Small perturbations, large consequences.** At bifurcation points, the system is maximally sensitive to fluctuation. A tiny perturbation that would be damped out in a near-equilibrium system is amplified into a macroscopic change in a far-from-equilibrium system. This is the physical mechanism for the amplification of novelty.
- **The arrow of time is creative.** Prigogine rejects the view that the universe is running down toward heat death. In far-from-equilibrium conditions — which characterize the biosphere, ecosystems, and human societies — the arrow of time points toward increasing organization and complexity. Entropy production is not merely destructive; it is the engine of self-organization.

## Methodology

Theoretical physics and physical chemistry, supported by mathematical formalism and experimental demonstrations. Prigogine draws on nonlinear thermodynamics, the mathematics of bifurcation theory, and experimental examples from fluid dynamics (Benard cells), chemistry (Belousov-Zhabotinsky oscillating reactions), and biology (morphogenesis). The book is written for a general audience but is grounded in Prigogine's decades of formal work on nonequilibrium thermodynamics, for which he received the Nobel Prize. Key mathematical tools include nonlinear differential equations, Lyapunov stability analysis, and stochastic processes near bifurcation points.

## Key Quotes

> "The laws of nature are not deterministic in the classical sense. Near equilibrium, the world is predictable. Far from equilibrium, at the bifurcation point, the world becomes creative — small fluctuations are amplified into new organizational states that could not have been predicted."

> "We see that far from equilibrium, matter acquires new properties. What appears as 'waste' — the irreversible dissipation of energy — becomes the very source of order."

> "The future is not given. It is under perpetual construction. The arrow of time is not merely a slide toward disorder but the engine of an ongoing creation."

## Limitations

The leap from physical dissipative structures (Benard cells, chemical oscillations) to cognitive and creative processes is metaphorical rather than rigorously demonstrated. Prigogine himself was cautious about extending thermodynamic concepts to mind and culture, though he acknowledged the suggestiveness of the parallel. The book's arguments about the foundations of physics (the "end of certainty," the fundamental nature of irreversibility) remain contested among physicists — the interpretation of time's arrow is still an active debate. The general-audience writing sacrifices mathematical precision, and some physicists have criticized the book for overstating the philosophical implications of the science. The specific claim that biological and cognitive creativity instantiate the same dynamics as physical dissipative structures requires additional bridging theory that Prigogine does not provide.
