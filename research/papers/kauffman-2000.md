# Investigations

**Author(s):** Stuart A. Kauffman (Santa Fe Institute; Bios Group)
**Published:** Oxford University Press, 2000
**ISBN:** 0-19-512104-X
**Field:** Complexity Science

---

## Abstract

In *Investigations*, Kauffman extends the conceptual framework of *At Home in the Universe* into a broader philosophical and scientific inquiry. The central questions: What are the laws that govern the biosphere's persistent expansion into novelty? Can we formulate a "fourth law of thermodynamics" for self-constructing systems? Kauffman argues that autonomous agents — systems that can both reproduce and perform at least one thermodynamic work cycle — persistently explore and expand the adjacent possible. Unlike the physical universe, which tends toward entropy under the second law of thermodynamics, the biosphere builds up toward greater complexity, diversity, and organizational depth. This expansion is both unpredictable in its specifics and lawful in its dynamics. Kauffman proposes that we cannot prestate the configuration space of the biosphere — the space of possible organisms, ecosystems, and technologies. The adjacent possible is not a fixed landscape to be explored; it is a landscape that expands as it is explored. Each novel entity that comes into existence creates new adjacent possibles that did not exist before.

## Key Findings

- **The biosphere expands into the adjacent possible.** The history of life is not the exploration of a fixed fitness landscape but the persistent expansion into an ever-growing adjacent possible. Each new species, adaptation, or technology creates new niches, new selection pressures, and new possibilities for further innovation. The space of the possible grows as it is explored.
- **We cannot prestate the adjacent possible.** For physical systems, we can (in principle) enumerate all possible microstates. For the biosphere, we cannot. Kauffman argues this is a fundamental asymmetry: biological and cultural evolution cannot be predicted because the space of possibilities is not enumerable in advance. The swim bladder of a fish could not have been predicted from first principles — yet once it exists, it opens entirely new adaptive possibilities (buoyancy control, eventually lungs).
- **Autonomous agents and self-construction.** Kauffman defines an autonomous agent as a self-reproducing system that performs at least one thermodynamic work cycle — it does work to maintain and propagate itself. Autonomous agents are the units that explore the adjacent possible. Importantly, they create the conditions for further exploration: an organism that evolves a new capability changes the environment for all other organisms.
- **The universe is not running down — it is building up.** While the second law of thermodynamics describes the increase of entropy in closed systems, the biosphere is an open system maintained by the throughflow of solar energy. In this far-from-equilibrium context, the direction is not toward disorder but toward increasing complexity, diversity, and organizational depth. This is the constructive direction of evolution.
- **Co-creation of the possible.** Kauffman argues that genuinely new possibilities come into existence through the creative acts of autonomous agents. The possible is not eternal and pre-given — it is partially constructed by the agents that explore it. This has profound implications for creativity: the creative act does not discover pre-existing possibilities; it creates new ones.

## Methodology

Philosophical and theoretical. *Investigations* is structured as a series of thought experiments, analogies, and conceptual arguments rather than formal models or empirical data. Kauffman draws on examples from molecular biology (autocatalytic sets, the origin of life), evolutionary biology (the Cambrian explosion, exaptation), technology (the evolution of the screwdriver, the jet engine), and economic systems (technological innovation, niche creation). The book attempts to formulate candidate laws for self-constructing systems, though Kauffman acknowledges these are proto-theories rather than established science.

## Relevance to Spark Mode

**Principle:** #7 — Emergence
**Specific connection:** *Investigations* provides the deepest theoretical justification for Spark Mode's treatment of Emergence as genuinely novel — not mere recombination of existing elements, but the creation of something that expands the possible. The specification's distinction between novelty (new combination of existing elements) and emergence (something that creates new adjacent possibles) maps directly to Kauffman's argument. When Spark Mode flags true emergence, it is identifying moments where the output is not just a novel arrangement of the inputs but a configuration that opens new possibilities that did not exist before the creative act. The swim bladder metaphor applies: a genuinely emergent creative output changes what is subsequently possible.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The philosophical depth beneath the adjacent possible concept. *At Home in the Universe* introduced the adjacent possible as a feature of complex adaptive systems. *Investigations* extends it into a general principle: the possible is not fixed, it is constructed through the activity of autonomous agents. Applied to creativity: the creative person is not exploring a pre-existing space of possible ideas — they are partially constructing that space through the act of creation. This is the strongest argument against the "nothing is truly original" position and the strongest theoretical foundation for genuine emergence.

## Key Quotes

> "We live in a universe that is not only stranger than we suppose, but stranger than we can suppose — because the space of the possible is not fixed but grows as it is explored."

> "The biosphere does not merely explore a pre-given space of possibilities. It creates the space as it goes. The adjacent possible expands as it is entered."

> "We cannot prestate the becoming of the biosphere. We can describe what has happened. We can sometimes see what is adjacent. But we cannot say what will be, because what will be partially depends on what is created — and creation is not deducible."

## Limitations

*Investigations* is Kauffman's most philosophical and least formal book. The "fourth law of thermodynamics" he proposes remains speculative and has not been formalized or empirically tested. The concept that the adjacent possible "expands as it is explored" is intuitively compelling but difficult to operationalize or falsify. Critics have noted that the book relies heavily on analogy (from biology to technology to economics to creativity) without rigorous demonstration that the same dynamics apply across all these domains. The writing is dense and at times repetitive. Some arguments are circular: the claim that genuinely new possibilities come into existence through creative acts is difficult to distinguish from the claim that we simply failed to foresee those possibilities from the prior state.
