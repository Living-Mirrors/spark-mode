# Investigations

**Author:** Stuart A. Kauffman, Santa Fe Institute
**Published:** Oxford University Press, 2000. ISBN: 978-0-19-512104-9
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1093/oso/9780195121049.001.0001
**Field:** Complexity Science, Theoretical Biology, Philosophy of Science

---

## Abstract

*Investigations* extends Kauffman's earlier work on self-organization into a broader philosophical and scientific inquiry into the nature of autonomous agents, the biosphere, and the emergence of genuine novelty. The book introduces the concept of an "autonomous agent" — a system that can perform thermodynamic work cycles and reproduce — and argues that the biosphere's capacity to generate novelty is not fully describable by any finite set of laws. Kauffman proposes that the universe is not merely rearranging existing elements but "ceaselessly creating genuine novelty" — an argument that has direct implications for understanding creative emergence.

## Key Findings

- **The autonomous agent.** Kauffman defines a minimum autonomous agent as a system that can: (a) perform at least one thermodynamic work cycle; (b) reproduce; and (c) evolve. This framework provides a formal definition of what it means for a system to generate novelty rather than merely rearranging existing components.
- **The non-prestateability of the biosphere.** The biosphere creates genuinely new configurations that could not have been listed in advance. Kauffman argues that this "non-prestateability" — the inability to specify in advance all possible future states — is a fundamental property of sufficiently complex systems. Creative emergence is an instance of this: the genuinely new idea could not have been predicted from any analysis of the inputs.
- **The expanding adjacent possible.** As a system explores its adjacent possible (the set of next reachable states), it changes the adjacent possible itself. Each new configuration opens new possibilities that did not exist before. Applied to creativity: each creative act changes the landscape of what is creatable next. The creative process is not exploration of a fixed space but expansion of the space itself.
- **Beyond law.** Kauffman argues that the emergence of genuine novelty in the biosphere (and by extension, in creative cognition) cannot be fully captured by law-like regularities. Conditions can be specified (edge of chaos, sufficient complexity, autonomous agency), but the specific form of emergence cannot be predicted. This is not ignorance — it is a fundamental property of emergent systems.

## Methodology

Theoretical and philosophical investigation combining formal modeling (extensions of the Boolean network and autocatalytic set frameworks from *At Home in the Universe*), thought experiments, and synthesis across biology, physics, and philosophy of science. The book is more speculative and philosophical than its predecessor, developing the implications of self-organization theory for questions about the nature of life, agency, and novelty.

## Relevance to Spark Mode

**Principle:** #7 — Emergence
**Specific connection:** *Investigations* provides the theoretical argument for why emergence cannot be forced — only enabled. The concept of the "expanding adjacent possible" maps directly to the specification's treatment of the Phase Marker (◆): genuine emergence changes the possibility space itself. The non-prestateability argument grounds the specification's insistence that the Phase Marker is the rarest output — if emergence could be predicted or commanded, it would not be genuine emergence.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The philosophical depth behind the emergence principle. Where *At Home in the Universe* provides the mathematical framework, *Investigations* provides the conceptual argument for why genuine novelty is fundamentally unpredictable — and why this unpredictability is not a failure of the model but a property of the phenomenon.

## Key Quotes

> "The biosphere is persistently constructing itself into novelty. It is not merely rearranging a fixed set of components — it is creating new components, new interactions, new possibilities that did not and could not exist before."

> "The adjacent possible is not a fixed space waiting to be explored. Each exploration changes the space itself. The creative act does not find novelty — it creates the terrain on which the next novelty will arise."

> "We cannot prestate the configuration space of the biosphere. This is not a statement about our ignorance. It is a statement about the nature of the system."

## Limitations

*Investigations* is significantly more speculative than *At Home in the Universe.* The arguments about non-prestateability and "beyond law" have been criticized as unfalsifiable — if emergence is by definition unpredictable, how can the theory be tested? The application to creative cognition is entirely by analogy; Kauffman does not study human creativity. The autonomous agent framework, while formally defined, has not been broadly adopted in creativity research. Some reviewers have noted that the philosophical ambitions of the book exceed its scientific rigor.
