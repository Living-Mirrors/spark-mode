# The Structure of Creative Cognition in the Human Brain

**Authors:** Rex E. Jung, Brittany S. Mead, Jessica Carrasco, Ranee A. Flores; Department of Neurosurgery and Department of Psychology, University of New Mexico
**Published:** *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*, 7, Article 330, 2013
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.3389/fnhum.2013.00330
**Field:** Cognitive Neuroscience, Creativity Research, Neuroimaging

---

## Abstract

This paper reviews structural and functional neuroimaging studies of creative cognition, synthesizing findings from diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), voxel-based morphometry (VBM), and functional MRI (fMRI). The authors propose a model in which creative cognition depends on the interaction between "blind variation" (the generation of novel ideas, associated with reduced white matter integrity in prefrontal regions and increased Default Mode Network activation) and "selective retention" (the evaluation of ideas, associated with executive control regions). The paper argues for a "disinhibition" model: creative ideation requires relaxation of executive control, allowing broader and more spontaneous patterns of neural activation.

## Key Findings

- **The Default Mode Network in creative cognition.** The DMN (medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate cortex, angular gyrus, hippocampus) is consistently activated during creative ideation tasks. Contrary to earlier characterizations of the DMN as the brain "idling," the authors argue it performs active recombination of stored experiences and concepts.
- **The disinhibition hypothesis.** Creative individuals show reduced integrity of white matter tracts in frontal regions (measured by DTI), which the authors interpret as reduced top-down executive control during ideation. This is functionally equivalent to Martindale's (1995) defocused attention model — reduced executive filtering allows more elements to be simultaneously active.
- **Structural brain differences in creative individuals.** Voxel-based morphometry studies show that creative individuals have distinct patterns of cortical thickness and gray matter volume in prefrontal, temporal, and parietal regions — though the specific patterns vary across studies and creativity measures.
- **The blind variation and selective retention (BVSR) neural model.** Creative cognition involves two complementary neural processes: (a) broad, disinhibited activation (blind variation, DMN-dominant) generates candidate ideas; (b) focused, executive activation (selective retention, ECN-dominant) evaluates and selects among them. Creative expertise involves the ability to switch between these modes fluidly.

## Methodology

Systematic review of structural and functional neuroimaging studies of creativity (approximately 45 studies reviewed). The paper synthesizes findings across multiple imaging modalities (fMRI, DTI, VBM) and multiple creativity measures (divergent thinking tasks, Remote Associates Test, real-world creative achievement). No new empirical data are presented — this is a review and theoretical synthesis.

## Relevance to Spark Mode

**Principle:** #6 — Incubation
**Specific connection:** Jung's review provides the neuroscientific basis for incubation: the DMN performs active recombination during states of reduced executive control. The specification's Release Signals (⟐) flag moments when executive effort is counterproductive — when the ECN's evaluative filtering is preventing the DMN's generative recombination. The disinhibition hypothesis explains why "trying harder" sometimes makes creative problems worse: increased effort increases executive control, which narrows the activation landscape and prevents the broad, spontaneous recombination that incubation enables.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** A comprehensive neural model integrating structural and functional imaging findings. Where Martindale (1995) provided EEG evidence for defocused attention, Jung synthesizes a decade of fMRI and DTI studies into a coherent model of the creative brain's architecture.

## Key Quotes

> "Creative cognition appears to depend not on the strength of executive control but on its strategic relaxation — the ability to reduce top-down filtering when generation is more valuable than evaluation."

> "The Default Mode Network is not the brain at rest. It is the brain at play — recombining the elements of experience into novel configurations."

## Limitations

This is a review paper, not an empirical study. The reviewed studies use diverse creativity measures (divergent thinking, RAT, self-report creative achievement) that may not be measuring the same construct. The disinhibition hypothesis, while supported by DTI data, is based on correlational evidence — reduced white matter integrity in creative individuals could reflect many factors beyond reduced executive control. The field of creativity neuroscience was relatively young in 2013, and many of the reviewed studies had small sample sizes (N < 30).
