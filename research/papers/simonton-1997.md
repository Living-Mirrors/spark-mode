# Creative Productivity: A Predictive and Explanatory Model of Career Trajectories and Landmarks

**Author:** Dean Keith Simonton, Department of Psychology, University of California, Davis
**Published:** *Psychological Review*, 104(1), 66–89, 1997
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.104.1.66
**Field:** Creativity Research, Mathematical Psychology

---

## Abstract

This paper presents a formal mathematical model of creative productivity across careers. Building on the "constant probability of success" model and the "equal-odds baseline," Simonton develops a two-step stochastic process: (1) creative ideation, governed by the size of the ideational pool and the rate of combination; and (2) creative elaboration, the process of developing raw ideas into finished works. The model predicts career trajectories — the characteristic peak and decline of creative output — and accounts for variation across domains (poets peak early, historians late) in terms of the size of the initial ideation pool and the elaboration rate.

## Key Findings

- **Two-step model.** Creative productivity involves (a) ideation (generating raw combinations from the pool of mental elements) and (b) elaboration (developing raw ideas into finished products). Both steps are stochastic. The overall output rate is a function of the ideation rate, the elaboration time, and the depletion/replenishment of the ideational pool.
- **Career trajectory prediction.** The model generates the characteristic inverted-U shape of creative careers: output rises as the creator builds expertise and begins combining, peaks when ideation rate and elaboration capacity are jointly maximized, and declines as the ideational pool is partially depleted. Domain-specific variation in peak age (e.g., mathematics peaks earlier than history) reflects differences in initial pool size and elaboration time.
- **The equal-odds baseline holds.** Across the career, the ratio of major to minor works remains constant. A creator's "best" years are simply their most prolific years.
- **Quantity predicts quality.** The model formalizes the empirical finding that the strongest predictor of a creator's number of high-impact works is their total number of works. This is not tautological — it reflects the stochastic nature of creative production.

## Methodology

Mathematical modeling with empirical validation. Simonton develops differential equations describing the ideation-elaboration process, derives predictions for career output curves, and tests these predictions against biographical data from 2,026 creators across multiple domains (scientists, composers, painters, poets, novelists, philosophers). Goodness-of-fit is assessed using age-specific productivity data aggregated across creators within each domain.

## Key Quotes

> "The probability that a given contribution will be a hit is a constant, p, that varies across creators but not within the career of a single creator. The expected number of hits is simply p times total output."

> "The most prolific periods of a creator's career are also the periods containing the highest proportion of masterworks — not because the creator is 'on a roll' but because more rolls of the dice produce more sixes."

## Limitations

The model assumes a largely fixed ideational pool, with replenishment occurring but not keeping pace with depletion. This may understate the role of late-career learning and domain-switching. The model also treats ideation as essentially random, which is contested (see Gabora, 2005). The data is aggregated across creators within domains, which smooths out individual variation that may be theoretically significant.
