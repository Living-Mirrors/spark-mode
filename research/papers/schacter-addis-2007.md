# Constructive Memory: The Ghosts of Past and Future

**Author(s):** Daniel L. Schacter (Harvard University), Donna Rose Addis (University of Auckland / Harvard University)
**Published:** *Nature*, 445, 27, 2007
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1038/445027a
**Field:** Cognitive Neuroscience / Memory

---

## Abstract

Recent neuroimaging evidence reveals a striking overlap between the brain regions engaged when people remember the past and when they imagine the future. The hippocampus, along with a network of medial temporal and prefrontal regions closely resembling the Default Mode Network, is activated both during episodic memory retrieval and during the construction of novel future scenarios. We propose the constructive episodic simulation hypothesis: the adaptive function of memory is not to faithfully reproduce the past but to flexibly recombine elements of past experience into simulations of possible future events. Memory, in this view, is not a recording device — it is a constructive system that disassembles experience into its constituent elements and reassembles them into novel configurations. The same neural machinery that reconstructs the past imagines the future.

## Key Findings

- **Memory and imagination share a neural substrate.** fMRI studies show that the hippocampus and a broader network of default mode regions (medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate, lateral temporal cortex) are activated both when participants recall past events and when they imagine novel future events. The overlap is extensive and consistent across studies.
- **Memory is constructive, not reproductive.** The hippocampus does not store complete episodes and replay them. Instead, it stores elements — people, places, objects, emotions, spatial layouts — and recombines them during retrieval. Every act of remembering is an act of reconstruction. This is why memory is prone to distortion: the system is optimized for flexible recombination, not faithful reproduction.
- **The constructive episodic simulation hypothesis.** The adaptive purpose of a constructive memory system is to enable the simulation of future events. By decomposing past experience into elements and recombining them, the system can generate scenarios that have never occurred — imagined futures, counterfactual pasts, hypothetical situations. This is the biological basis of creative imagination.
- **Amnesic patients cannot imagine the future.** Patients with hippocampal damage who cannot recall past episodes also cannot construct coherent imagined future scenarios. When asked to imagine a novel scene, they produce fragmented, impoverished descriptions lacking spatial coherence — the same deficit they show in memory. This double dissociation confirms that memory and imagination depend on the same constructive process.

## Methodology

This is a brief perspective/commentary piece in *Nature*, synthesizing findings from several empirical studies. The key evidence comes from: (a) fMRI studies comparing brain activity during past recall and future imagination in healthy adults; (b) neuropsychological case studies of patients with hippocampal amnesia (particularly patient K.C. and five cases studied by Hassabis et al.); (c) behavioral studies showing that the richness and detail of imagined future events is correlated with the richness of remembered past events.

## Key Quotes

> "The constructive nature of memory is not a design flaw but an adaptive feature. A memory system that faithfully recorded every detail of experience would be poorly suited to the creative task of imagining future possibilities."

> "Amnesic patients provide compelling evidence: those who cannot recollect the past also cannot imagine the future. The same constructive process underlies both capacities."

> "The hippocampus does not replay the past — it recombines its elements. Every act of remembering is, in a sense, an act of imagination."

## Limitations

This is a brief perspective piece (one page), not a full research article. The constructive episodic simulation hypothesis, while well-supported, is presented at a high level without detailed mechanistic specification. The amnesic patient evidence is compelling but based on small case series. The piece does not address individual differences in constructive ability or the role of executive control in shaping constructive simulations. More recent work (Beaty et al., 2015, 2018) has elaborated on how executive networks interact with the default mode/hippocampal system during creative production — a dimension this early piece does not address.
