# Constructive Memory: The Ghosts of Past and Future

**Authors:** Daniel L. Schacter, Department of Psychology, Harvard University; Donna Rose Addis, Department of Psychology, University of Auckland (now at University of Toronto)
**Published:** *Nature*, 445(7123), 27, 2007
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1038/445027a
**Field:** Cognitive Neuroscience, Memory, Prospection

---

## Abstract

This brief but influential commentary in *Nature* proposes that the constructive nature of memory — long viewed as a flaw — is actually an adaptive feature that supports future-oriented cognition. The hippocampus uses the same mechanism for both remembering the past and imagining the future: it deconstructs experiences into component elements and flexibly recombines them into novel configurations. This "constructive episodic simulation" hypothesis has profound implications for creative cognition: the same neural machinery that reconstructs memories also generates novel scenarios, making memory and imagination fundamentally the same operation applied to different temporal directions.

## Key Findings

- **Memory is not playback.** The hippocampus does not store and retrieve experiences as intact wholes. It stores elements (people, places, objects, emotions, temporal contexts) separately and reconstructs them at retrieval. This is why memories are malleable, subject to distortion, and influenced by current context — they are assembled on the fly from component parts.
- **Imagination uses the same mechanism.** When people imagine future events, the same hippocampal regions are activated as during episodic memory retrieval. The constructive process is identical: elements from past experiences are extracted and recombined into novel configurations. The difference is temporal direction, not cognitive mechanism.
- **The constructive memory system supports creativity.** If the hippocampus can flexibly recombine elements of experience into novel configurations, then creative imagination is a natural extension of normal memory function. The "ghosts of past and future" are made from the same material, reassembled by the same machinery.
- **The Default Mode Network as recombination engine.** The hippocampus is a core node of the DMN. During mind-wandering, daydreaming, and rest, the DMN performs spontaneous recombination of memory elements — producing novel scenarios, counterfactuals, and imagined futures. This is the neural basis of incubation.

## Methodology

Theoretical commentary synthesizing findings from neuroimaging studies of episodic memory and future thinking. Schacter and Addis cite fMRI studies showing overlapping hippocampal activation during memory retrieval and future imagination (Addis et al., 2007; Szpunar et al., 2007), neuropsychological case studies of patients with hippocampal damage who show parallel deficits in memory and imagination (Hassabis et al., 2007), and computational models of hippocampal pattern completion and pattern separation.

**Specific connection:** Schacter and Addis explain WHY the DMN is relevant to creative cognition: it runs the hippocampal recombination engine. During incubation, when executive control is released, this engine operates freely — deconstructing stored experiences and recombining them into novel configurations. This is the neural mechanism underlying Wallas's (1926) incubation stage. The specification's Release Signals (⟐) flag moments when this recombination engine should be allowed to run without interference.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The functional explanation of the DMN's role in creativity. Jung (2013) and Beaty (2015, 2018) show that the DMN is involved in creative cognition. Schacter and Addis explain what the DMN is *doing* — running a hippocampal recombination engine that deconstructs and reassembles experiential elements. Memory, imagination, and creativity are the same machinery applied to different purposes.

## Key Quotes

> "The constructive nature of memory is not a design flaw but an adaptive feature: the same system that reconstructs the past also constructs possible futures."

> "Memory and imagination are not opposites — they are the same cognitive operation pointed in different temporal directions."

## Limitations

This is a one-page commentary, not a full research paper. The constructive episodic simulation hypothesis has been developed more fully in subsequent publications (Schacter & Addis, 2007b; Schacter et al., 2012; Addis, 2018). The claim that memory and imagination share the same mechanism has been qualified by subsequent research showing partial dissociations — some brain regions are more active for imagination than memory and vice versa. The link to creativity specifically (as opposed to future thinking generally) is inferred but not directly tested in this paper.
