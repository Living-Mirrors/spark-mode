# The Process of Janusian Thinking in Creativity

**Author:** Albert Rothenberg (1930–2024), Department of Psychiatry, Yale University School of Medicine
**Published:** *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 24(3), 195–205, 1971
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1001/archpsyc.1971.01750090001001
**Field:** Psychiatry, Creativity Research, Psychodynamic Theory

---

## Abstract

This paper introduces the concept of "Janusian thinking" — the active, simultaneous conception of two or more opposite or antithetical ideas, images, or concepts. Named after the Roman god Janus, depicted with two faces looking in opposite directions, the concept is proposed as a central cognitive process in high-level creative achievement. Rothenberg draws on clinical interviews with creative writers and scientists, controlled word association experiments, and historical case analysis (Einstein, Bohr, Mozart) to argue that Janusian thinking is a specific, identifiable cognitive operation — distinct from ambivalence, paradox tolerance, or dialectical synthesis — that operates at the critical moments of creative breakthrough.

## Key Findings

- **Definition of Janusian thinking.** "Actively conceiving two or more opposite or antithetical ideas, images, or concepts simultaneously." The key properties: (a) the opposites are genuinely antithetical, not merely different; (b) they are held simultaneously, not sequentially; (c) the holding is active, not passive; (d) the process is generative, producing new ideas from the sustained tension.
- **Distinction from related concepts.** Janusian thinking is NOT: (a) tolerance of ambiguity (which is passive acceptance, not active generation); (b) dialectical synthesis (which resolves the contradiction, destroying the tension); (c) ambivalence (which is emotional, not cognitive); (d) paradox (which is a logical property of statements, not a cognitive operation).
- **Clinical evidence.** Interviews with prize-winning authors revealed that the breakthrough moments in their creative work consistently involved the simultaneous conception of opposites. The process was conscious and deliberate, not unconscious or accidental.
- **Controlled word association data.** In word association experiments, creative individuals (identified by independent achievement criteria) produced significantly more antithetical responses (responses opposite to the stimulus word) than matched non-creative controls (p < .01). This suggests Janusian thinking is a measurable cognitive disposition, not merely a retrospective description.
- **Historical case: Einstein.** Einstein conceived a man falling from a roof as simultaneously in motion (falling under gravity) and at rest (experiencing weightlessness). This simultaneous holding of opposites was the conceptual origin of the equivalence principle — the foundation of general relativity. Einstein did not resolve the contradiction; he held it, and the theory emerged.

## Methodology

Mixed methods: (1) Semi-structured interviews with creative writers (N = 12, all recipients of major literary prizes) focused on their creative process during specific works; (2) Controlled word association experiments comparing creative participants (identified by publication record and peer nomination) with non-creative matched controls (N = 54 total, 27 per group); (3) Historical case analysis of documented creative breakthroughs.

## Key Quotes

> "Janusian thinking consists of actively conceiving two or more opposite or antithetical ideas, images, or concepts simultaneously. It is a conscious, rational process, distinct from the more primitive co-existence of opposites found in dreaming and psychosis."

> "The creative person does not merely tolerate contradiction — he actively conceives it and uses it as a springboard for new creation."

> "Einstein's formulation of the equivalence principle began with the simultaneous conception that a man falling from a roof is both in motion and at rest."

## Limitations

The sample sizes are small (12 writers, 27 creative participants in the word association study). The clinical interview methodology relies on retrospective self-report, which may be reconstructive rather than accurate. The concept of "opposite" or "antithetical" is not always clearly operationalized — in some cases, the examples seem to involve contradiction, in others merely tension. The historical case analyses, while compelling, cannot establish that Janusian thinking was actually the cognitive process occurring (as opposed to a retrospective description).
