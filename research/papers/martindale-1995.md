# Creativity and Connectionism

**Author:** Colin Martindale (1943–2008), Department of Psychology, University of Maine
**Published:** In S. M. Smith, T. B. Ward, & R. A. Finke (Eds.), *The Creative Cognition Approach* (pp. 249–268). MIT Press, 1995. ISBN: 978-0-262-19354-9
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/2205.003.0015
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology, Computational Neuroscience, Creativity Research

---

## Abstract

This chapter develops a connectionist (neural network) model of creative cognition, grounding Mednick's (1962) associative theory in computational and neurophysiological terms. Martindale argues that creative ideation corresponds to a state of "defocused attention" — low cortical arousal that allows broadly distributed activation across neural networks. In this state, remote nodes that are normally below threshold become active, enabling novel combinations. The chapter presents EEG evidence that creative individuals show higher alpha wave activity (associated with low arousal and defocused attention) during creative tasks, and develops a formal connectionist model that simulates the relationship between arousal, attention breadth, and associative novelty.

## Key Findings

- **Defocused attention as creative mechanism.** Creative ideation does not require intense concentration — it requires its opposite. Defocused attention (low cortical arousal) broadens the activation gradient across neural networks, allowing more nodes to be simultaneously active and increasing the probability of remote associations.
- **EEG evidence.** During creative tasks (divergent thinking, RAT solving), individuals who produce more creative solutions show higher alpha wave activity (8–12 Hz), which is associated with relaxed, internally-directed attention. Beta wave activity (13–30 Hz), associated with focused external attention, correlates with less creative output.
- **The arousal theory of creativity.** Creative capacity is inversely related to cortical arousal during the generative phase. Too much arousal narrows attention to dominant associates (steep hierarchy). Too little arousal produces unfocused, incoherent activation. The optimum for creative generation is moderate-to-low arousal — enough activation for coherence, little enough for breadth.
- **Connectionist simulation.** Martindale's neural network model demonstrates that when the activation function allows broader spreading (simulating defocused attention), the network produces combinations that are more remote and more novel than when activation is concentrated (simulating focused attention). This provides computational proof-of-concept for Mednick's flat hierarchy.

## Methodology

Three strands: (1) EEG studies comparing cortical activation patterns during creative vs. non-creative tasks in undergraduate participants (N = 20–40 per study); (2) connectionist (neural network) simulations with varying activation parameters modeling different arousal states; (3) integration with existing creativity research, particularly Mednick's RAT studies and Eysenck's personality-creativity work.

## Relevance to Spark Mode

**Principle:** #3 — Remote Reaching
**Specific connection:** Martindale provides the neural mechanism for Mednick's flat hierarchy. The specification's emphasis on "resisting the pull of the obvious" and "maintaining activation across semantic distance" directly implements Martindale's defocused attention model. Remote Reaching is not effortful straining toward distant ideas — it is the deliberate relaxation of focus that allows distant ideas to remain active.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The bridge between Mednick's psychometric theory and neuroscience. Where Mednick described what the creative associative landscape looks like, Martindale explained how the brain produces it — through arousal modulation and defocused attention. This also connects forward to the DMN research of Jung (2013) and Beaty (2015, 2018).

## Key Quotes

> "Creative thinking is not hard thinking. It is, in a very real sense, easy thinking — thinking in a state of low arousal where the normally sharp boundaries between activated and non-activated concepts are softened."

> "The creative mind is not more powerful — it is more relaxed. And in that relaxation, more of the associative landscape becomes available."

## Limitations

The EEG methodology of the 1990s was limited in spatial resolution — alpha activity is a gross measure that doesn't specify which brain regions are involved. Subsequent fMRI work (Jung, 2013; Beaty, 2015) provided much finer spatial resolution. The connectionist model is simplified relative to actual neural architecture. The arousal theory has been qualified by subsequent research showing that creative tasks sometimes require high arousal (during evaluation) as well as low arousal (during generation) — the DMN-ECN coupling model of Beaty et al. (2018) captures this complexity better.
