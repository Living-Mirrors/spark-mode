# Creative Cognition: Theory, Research, and Applications

**Authors:** Thomas B. Ward, Steven M. Smith, Ronald A. Finke; Department of Psychology, Texas A&M University (Ward, Smith) and Department of Psychology, Texas A&M University (Finke)
**Published:** MIT Press, 1995. ISBN: 978-0-262-23181-1
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/2205.001.0001
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology, Creativity Research

---

## Abstract

*Creative Cognition* (also known as the "Geneplore" model) presents an experimentally grounded framework for creative thinking as a two-phase process: (1) **generative** processes that produce candidate ideas, mental images, or "preinventive structures," and (2) **exploratory** processes that interpret, elaborate, and evaluate these candidates. The book reports results from dozens of experiments using structured imagination tasks — participants generate novel concepts (e.g., alien creatures, inventions, furniture) under systematically varied conditions. The key finding: the novelty of creative output is a direct function of the dissimilarity of the input elements. When participants combine elements from dissimilar categories, the resulting concepts are rated as more creative than when elements come from similar categories. This provides experimental validation of Koestler's bisociation hypothesis.

## Key Findings

- **The Geneplore model.** Creative thinking alternates between two phases: (a) generation of "preinventive structures" — mental representations that are ambiguous, novel, and rich in potential interpretations; and (b) exploration of these structures — interpreting them, finding uses for them, elaborating their properties. Creative output emerges from the iterative cycling between generation and exploration.
- **Structured imagination.** When asked to create novel concepts (e.g., "design a creature from another planet"), people systematically draw on existing category knowledge. The resulting concepts are constrained by what the person already knows. This demonstrates that creative imagination is structured, not random — consistent with both Mednick's associative model and Koestler's matrix concept.
- **Category dissimilarity drives novelty.** In experiments where participants combine elements from specified categories to create novel inventions, the more dissimilar the source categories, the more novel and creative the resulting inventions (as rated by blind judges). This is the experimental operationalization of Koestler's claim that bisociation requires "habitually incompatible" frames.
- **Preinventive structures.** Before a creative product is fully formed, the mind generates intermediate representations that are ambiguous and multi-interpretable. These "preinventive structures" are the raw material of creative exploration. They are not the finished creative product — they are the material from which the product is discovered.

## Methodology

Experimental cognitive psychology. The book reports results from over 30 experiments conducted by the authors and their students. Typical paradigm: participants are given components (parts, features, category members) and asked to combine them into novel concepts under specified constraints. Outputs are rated for creativity, practicality, and originality by trained blind judges using consensual assessment (Amabile, 1982). Sample sizes range from 30 to 120 per experiment. The paradigm allows systematic manipulation of variables including category distance, number of components, time pressure, and constraint type.

## Key Quotes

> "Creative products arise not from unconstrained imagination but from the structured combination of elements drawn from existing knowledge. The novelty of the output is a function of the dissimilarity of the inputs."

> "Preinventive structures are the mind's rough drafts — ambiguous, incomplete, but rich with potential. The creative act is as much about exploring these structures as it is about generating them."

## Limitations

The experimental paradigm uses relatively simple creative tasks (designing aliens, inventing gadgets) performed by undergraduate participants over short time periods (minutes to hours). Whether the Geneplore model applies to extended, expert creative work (composing a symphony, developing a scientific theory) is argued by analogy but not directly tested. The consensual assessment technique, while standard, relies on judges' implicit definitions of "creativity," which may not match the theoretical definitions being tested.
