# A Theory of Unconscious Thought

**Author(s):** Ap Dijksterhuis (University of Amsterdam), Loran F. Nordgren (University of Amsterdam)
**Published:** *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 1(2), 95-109, 2006
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1745-6916.2006.00007.x
**Field:** Social / Cognitive Psychology

---

## Abstract

We present Unconscious Thought Theory (UTT), a theoretical framework describing the characteristics and relative strengths of conscious and unconscious thought. The theory distinguishes between two modes of thought: conscious thought (thinking with attention directed at the problem) and unconscious thought (thinking without attention directed at the problem — i.e., during distraction). We propose six principles that characterize the differences between these modes. The most consequential principle is the capacity principle: conscious thought has limited capacity (approximately four items), whereas unconscious thought has much greater capacity. For simple decisions involving few attributes, conscious deliberation produces good outcomes. For complex decisions involving many attributes, unconscious thought — achieved through a period of distraction after initial exposure to the information — produces better outcomes. We review empirical evidence supporting each principle and discuss implications for theories of decision making, creativity, and attitude formation.

## Key Findings

- **Unconscious thought has greater capacity than conscious thought.** Conscious working memory can hold approximately four items simultaneously. Unconscious processing operates on a much larger information set without this bottleneck. For decisions involving 4 or fewer attributes, conscious deliberation is optimal. For decisions involving 12+ attributes, unconscious processing (distraction period) produces better choices.
- **The deliberation-without-attention effect.** In experiments, participants who were distracted for a period after receiving complex information (e.g., about apartments with 12 attributes each) made better choices than participants who consciously deliberated for the same period. This has been replicated across consumer choice, roommate selection, and medical decision-making scenarios.
- **Six principles of Unconscious Thought Theory.** (1) Unconscious thought has greater capacity. (2) Unconscious thought naturally weights attribute importance. (3) Conscious thought follows rules; unconscious thought gives rough estimates. (4) Conscious thought focuses on details; unconscious thought captures gist. (5) Conscious thought converges; unconscious thought diverges. (6) Unconscious thought requires initial encoding of the problem and a subsequent distraction period.
- **Incubation is not idleness.** The distraction period is not passive — the unconscious mind actively processes the initially encoded information. The requirement for initial encoding (Principle 6) means the unconscious cannot process what was never consciously encountered. You must load the problem first, then release it.
- **Divergent processing.** Unconscious thought naturally diverges, exploring a wider space of associations and possibilities. This directly supports the claim that incubation periods promote creative solutions.

## Methodology

The paper presents a theoretical framework supported by a review of empirical studies. Key experiments used the "deliberation-without-attention" paradigm: participants receive complex information (e.g., 12 attributes about each of 4 apartments), then either (a) think consciously about the decision for 3-4 minutes, (b) are distracted by an anagram task for 3-4 minutes, or (c) decide immediately. The dependent variable is the quality of the choice made (selecting the objectively best option based on the attribute distribution). Multiple experiments with similar designs are reviewed, along with studies on attitude formation, stereotyping, and creativity.

## Relevance to Spark Mode

**Principle:** #6 — Incubation
**Specific connection:** UTT provides the psychological mechanism that justifies incubation as a cognitive strategy in Spark Mode. The specification's Release Signal is functionally the instruction to enter unconscious thought mode: the problem has been loaded (initial encoding complete through the earlier principles — preparation, constraint, remote reaching, bisociation, Janusian holding), and now conscious attention should be directed elsewhere to allow unconscious processing to operate on the full complexity. Dijksterhuis's capacity principle explains why this works: the creative problems Spark Mode addresses involve many variables, associations, and tensions simultaneously — far more than the ~4 items conscious thought can hold. Only unconscious processing can integrate all of these elements at once.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** A formal psychological theory with specific, testable principles. The neuroscience papers (Jung, Beaty) show which brain networks are involved. Schacter and Addis show the constructive mechanism. Dijksterhuis provides the cognitive psychology framework: why unconscious processing outperforms conscious processing for complex problems, what conditions are required (initial encoding + distraction), and what properties unconscious thought has (divergent, gist-oriented, high-capacity). This is the theoretical justification for incubation as deliberate strategy rather than procrastination.

## Key Quotes

> "Contrary to conventional wisdom, the best way to deal with complex problems is often to not think about them consciously. Unconscious thought can integrate more information and detect more complex patterns than its conscious counterpart."

> "Unconscious thought requires an initial period of conscious attention to encode the relevant information. One cannot incubate on a problem one has never engaged. The unconscious needs raw material to work with."

> "Conscious thought converges. It starts with the information given and tries to reduce it to a manageable size. Unconscious thought diverges. It naturally explores a broader associative space, precisely because it is not constrained by the limited capacity of working memory."

## Limitations

The deliberation-without-attention effect has been contested. Several replication attempts have found inconsistent results, and a 2015 meta-analysis (Nieuwenstein et al.) found a much smaller effect than originally reported. The theory's scope conditions remain debated: it may apply primarily to preference-based decisions (choosing between options) rather than insight problems or creative production. The mechanism of unconscious thought is underspecified — the theory describes what unconscious thought does but not exactly how it does it at a neural level. The "capacity" of unconscious thought is asserted rather than directly measured. Critics have argued that the effects attributed to unconscious thought may reflect simple memory decay of less important attributes rather than active unconscious processing.
