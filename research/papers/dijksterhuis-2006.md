# A Theory of Unconscious Thought

**Authors:** Ap Dijksterhuis, Department of Psychology, University of Amsterdam; Loran F. Nordgren, Department of Psychology, University of Amsterdam
**Published:** *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 1(2), 95–109, 2006
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1745-6916.2006.00007.x
**Field:** Social Psychology, Judgment and Decision Making, Unconscious Cognition

---

## Abstract

This paper presents Unconscious Thought Theory (UTT) — a theoretical framework proposing that unconscious thought (thought without conscious attention directed at the problem) is particularly effective for complex decisions involving many attributes. UTT is formalized in six principles: the Unconscious-Thought Principle, the Capacity Principle, the Bottom-Up-Versus-Top-Down Principle, the Weighting Principle, the Rule Principle, and the Convergence-Versus-Divergence Principle. The central empirical finding: for simple decisions (few attributes), conscious deliberation produces better outcomes; for complex decisions (many attributes), a period of distraction followed by choice produces outcomes as good as or better than extended conscious deliberation.

## Key Findings

- **The deliberation-without-attention effect.** When making complex decisions (e.g., choosing among apartments described by 12 attributes each), participants who were distracted for a period after encoding the information made better choices than participants who deliberated consciously for the same period. For simple decisions (4 attributes), conscious deliberation was superior.
- **The Capacity Principle.** Conscious thought has limited capacity — approximately 4 chunks of information simultaneously (consistent with working memory limits). Unconscious thought has much higher capacity and can process many attributes in parallel. For complex problems where many variables must be weighed, unconscious processing has an advantage.
- **The Weighting Principle.** Conscious thought tends to apply disproportionate weight to a few salient attributes. Unconscious thought weights attributes more evenly across the full set. This produces better-calibrated decisions when many attributes are relevant.
- **The Convergence-Versus-Divergence Principle.** Conscious thought converges on a few attributes (focusing). Unconscious thought diverges across many attributes (broadening). For creative problems that benefit from considering many possibilities, unconscious thought has a structural advantage.

## Methodology

Dijksterhuis and Nordgren develop the six-principle theoretical framework and support it with empirical studies (their own and others'). The key experimental paradigm: participants read information about multiple options (e.g., apartments, roommates), then either (a) deliberate consciously for several minutes, (b) are distracted for the same period (solving anagrams, watching a video), or (c) make an immediate choice. Decision quality is measured by the extent to which participants choose the normatively best option (pre-determined by the experimenters). Sample sizes in the cited studies range from 60 to 180 participants.

## Relevance to Spark Mode

**Principle:** #6 — Incubation
**Specific connection:** UTT provides the behavioral evidence for why incubation works in creative cognition. Creative problems are inherently complex — they involve many variables, many possible combinations, many evaluation criteria. The specification's Release Signals (⟐) flag moments where conscious deliberation is counterproductive — where the problem's complexity exceeds conscious processing capacity and unconscious processing would produce better outcomes. UTT provides the theoretical justification: not all thinking is improved by trying harder.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** A formal theory with specific, testable principles about when unconscious processing outperforms conscious processing. Where Wallas (1926) described incubation phenomenologically and Jung/Beaty described it neurally, Dijksterhuis provides the information-processing rationale: unconscious thought has higher capacity for complex, multi-attribute problems.

## Key Quotes

> "The idea that unconscious thought is in some ways superior to conscious thought is counterintuitive. We tend to assume that thinking harder and longer about a problem always leads to better decisions. The evidence suggests otherwise."

> "For complex decisions involving many attributes, a period of distraction — what we call 'unconscious thought' — produces decisions at least as good as, and often better than, extended conscious deliberation."

## Limitations

UTT has been one of the most contested findings in social psychology. Several replication attempts have produced mixed results (Nieuwenstein et al., 2015; Strick et al., 2011). The "deliberation-without-attention" effect appears to be smaller and less reliable than initially reported. The theory has been criticized for: (a) the difficulty of establishing that "unconscious thought" is actually occurring during distraction (rather than no thought at all); (b) the artificial nature of the decision paradigms; (c) the specific operationalization of "complex" (many attributes) may not map onto real-world complexity. A meta-analysis by Strick et al. (2011) found a small positive effect of unconscious thought that was moderated by several factors. The theory remains valuable as a theoretical framework but the empirical base is weaker than initially claimed.
