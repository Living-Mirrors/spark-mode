# Origins of Genius: Darwinian Perspectives on Creativity

**Author:** Dean Keith Simonton, Department of Psychology, University of California, Davis
**Published:** Oxford University Press, 1999. ISBN: 978-0-19-512879-6
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1093/acprof:oso/9780195128796.001.0001
**Field:** Creativity Research, Evolutionary Psychology, Scientometrics

---

## Abstract

*Origins of Genius* extends Simonton's earlier work into a comprehensive Darwinian theory of creativity. The central argument: creative thought operates by the same logic as biological evolution — blind variation and selective retention (BVSR). Mental elements (ideas, concepts, images, associations) undergo essentially random recombination (blind variation), and the resulting combinations are then evaluated against criteria of usefulness, beauty, truth, or other domain-relevant standards (selective retention). The "genius" is not the person with the best ideas but the person with the richest supply of elements and the highest rate of combinatorial activity.

## Key Findings

- **Blind variation and selective retention (BVSR).** Creative thought is not directed search toward a known goal — it is stochastic combination of existing elements. The "blindness" is not total randomness but a process that generates combinations not predictable from the inputs. Selection happens after generation, not during it.
- **The constant-probability-of-success model.** A creator's probability of producing a major work on any given attempt remains constant across their career. Career peaks and valleys reflect changes in output rate, not changes in creative ability. More output = more hits, at a constant ratio.
- **Cross-domain breadth as evolutionary advantage.** Breadth across domains increases the pool of mental elements available for recombination. This is the creative equivalent of genetic diversity — more diversity = more potential for novel combinations = higher probability of adaptive solutions.
- **The Darwinian critique of "genius."** Genius is not a cognitive state qualitatively different from normal thought. It is normal combinatorial thought operating on a richer substrate at a higher rate. The mystification of genius obscures the actual mechanism.

## Methodology

Theoretical synthesis integrating historiometric data (from Simonton's earlier empirical work), evolutionary theory (Campbell's BVSR framework), and cognitive psychology. The book develops formal mathematical models of creative productivity and tests them against biographical and bibliometric data from scientists, composers, artists, and writers.

## Key Quotes

> "Creative genius operates by a Darwinian process of blind variation and selective retention. The variations are 'blind' in the sense that the creator cannot predict in advance which combinations will prove adaptive."

> "The genius, according to this model, is not someone who thinks better — but someone who thinks more, from a richer base."

## Limitations

The BVSR model has been criticized for overstating the "blindness" of creative variation. Gabora (2005) argues that creative search is more directed than Simonton allows — that context, goals, and heuristics guide the combinatorial process. Simonton (2013) later qualified "blind" to mean "sighted but not clairvoyant" — the creator uses heuristics but cannot predict outcomes. The debate remains active. Additionally, the evolutionary analogy has limits: biological evolution has no foresight, while human creators sometimes deliberately search for specific types of combinations.
