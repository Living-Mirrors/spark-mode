# The Creative Mind: Myths and Mechanisms

**Author(s):** Margaret A. Boden (University of Sussex, Department of Cognitive Science)
**Published:** Routledge, 2nd edition, 2004
**ISBN:** 0-415-31453-4
**Field:** AI / Cognitive Science

---

## Abstract

Margaret Boden proposes a computational framework for understanding creativity, arguing against the mysterian view that creativity is inexplicable and against the romantic view that it is uniquely human and unanalyzable. She distinguishes three types of creativity: combinational creativity (novel combinations of familiar ideas — jokes, poetic metaphors, collages), exploratory creativity (systematic exploration of a conceptual space, pushing its boundaries while remaining within its rules — as in a musician composing within a genre), and transformational creativity (altering the rules of a conceptual space itself, so that thoughts become possible that were previously inconceivable — as in the shift from tonal to atonal music, or from Euclidean to non-Euclidean geometry). Boden argues that all three types can be characterized computationally: a conceptual space is defined by a set of generative rules, and creativity involves either operating within that space (combinational, exploratory) or modifying its defining rules (transformational). This framework makes creativity amenable to scientific study and, in principle, to computational modeling.

## Key Findings

- **Three types of creativity.** (1) Combinational: making unfamiliar combinations of familiar ideas. The novelty lies in the juxtaposition, not the elements. Koestler's bisociation and Mednick's remote association are primarily combinational. (2) Exploratory: exploring the boundaries and possibilities of an established conceptual space. The creator works within accepted rules but finds previously unnoticed regions of the space. Much of normal science and craft mastery is exploratory. (3) Transformational: changing the rules that define a conceptual space, opening up possibilities that were previously impossible — not just unlikely, but structurally impossible under the prior rules. This is the deepest form of creativity.
- **Conceptual spaces are computationally definable.** A conceptual space (e.g., "tonal music," "Euclidean geometry," "impressionist painting") can be described as a set of generative rules and constraints. This makes it possible to specify what counts as "within" the space, "at the boundary" of the space, and "transforming" the space. Creativity becomes analyzable rather than mystical.
- **Transformational creativity is the rarest and most significant.** Combinational and exploratory creativity produce novelty within an existing framework. Transformational creativity changes the framework. Examples: Schoenberg's atonality (dropping the constraint of tonal center), Lobachevsky's non-Euclidean geometry (dropping the parallel postulate), Crick and Watson's DNA model (introducing a new conceptual space for heredity). In each case, dropping or modifying a constraint opened up a previously inconceivable region of idea-space.
- **Creativity can be computationally modeled.** Boden argues that AI systems can, in principle, exhibit all three types of creativity. Combinational creativity is already demonstrated by programs that generate jokes, analogies, and musical compositions. Exploratory creativity is demonstrated by systems that systematically explore a defined space (e.g., chess programs, style-based music generators). Transformational creativity is the hardest to model but is not in principle impossible — it requires a system that can modify its own generative rules.
- **P-creativity vs. H-creativity.** Boden distinguishes between psychological creativity (P-creativity: novel to the individual mind) and historical creativity (H-creativity: novel in the entire history of a culture). The cognitive processes are the same; the difference is in scope of novelty. This distinction is important for AI: a system can be P-creative (generating outputs novel to itself) without being H-creative (generating outputs novel to all of human history).

## Methodology

Philosophical and theoretical analysis, drawing on case studies from art, science, mathematics, and AI. Boden analyzes historical examples of creative breakthroughs (Kekule's benzene ring, Schoenberg's atonality, Darwin's natural selection) through the lens of her three-type framework. She also reviews AI creativity systems (Harold Cohen's AARON, David Cope's EMI, various computational joke generators) as existence proofs for different types of computational creativity. The book builds on Boden's earlier work in philosophy of mind and AI, particularly her 1990 first edition, updated with new AI examples and refined theoretical arguments.

## Key Quotes

> "Transformational creativity is not just a matter of doing new things within an existing conceptual space. It is a matter of changing the space itself — dropping a constraint, adding a dimension, transforming a rule — so that thoughts become possible which were literally inconceivable before."

> "To say that creativity is beyond scientific understanding is not a mark of respect for creativity. It is a counsel of despair. If we want to understand how creative minds work, we need to describe the generative processes involved — and those processes are, in principle, computational."

> "The most important creative ideas in human history did not merely explore existing conceptual spaces. They transformed them. And transformation requires not just intelligence or knowledge but the ability to identify and modify the very constraints that define a way of thinking."

## Limitations

Boden's framework is primarily descriptive rather than mechanistic — it classifies types of creativity but does not specify the neural or cognitive processes that produce them. The computational metaphor (conceptual spaces as rule-defined generative systems) may be too rigid: real conceptual spaces are often vague, overlapping, and contextually defined in ways that resist formalization. The distinction between exploratory and transformational creativity can be difficult to apply in practice: when is a modification within the space vs. a change to the space? Boden acknowledges this boundary is sometimes unclear. The AI examples from the 2004 edition are dated — modern large language models and generative AI raise new questions about computational creativity that the book does not address. The book focuses primarily on Western cultural examples.
