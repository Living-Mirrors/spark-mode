# The Creative Mind: Myths and Mechanisms

**Author:** Margaret A. Boden, Department of Cognitive Science, University of Sussex
**Published:** Routledge, 2004 (2nd edition; 1st edition 1990). ISBN: 978-0-415-31453-4
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.4324/9780203508527
**Field:** Cognitive Science, Artificial Intelligence, Creativity Research

---

## Abstract

*The Creative Mind* presents a computational-cognitive framework for understanding creativity. Boden argues that creativity can be scientifically analyzed without being "explained away" — understanding the mechanisms of creativity does not diminish its value. The book's central contribution is a taxonomy of three types of creativity: (1) combinational creativity (novel combinations of familiar ideas), (2) exploratory creativity (exploring the boundaries of an existing conceptual space), and (3) transformational creativity (transforming the conceptual space itself — changing the rules, not just the moves). The book argues that all three types can in principle be modeled computationally, and examines historical examples and AI systems through this lens.

## Key Findings

- **Three types of creativity.**
  1. **Combinational:** Novel combinations of familiar ideas. Example: a new metaphor combining two known concepts. This is the most common type and corresponds to Mednick's remote association and much of what Koestler calls bisociation.
  2. **Exploratory:** Exploring the boundaries and implications of an existing conceptual space (a style, a paradigm, a set of rules). Example: a jazz musician exploring the full possibilities of a chord progression. The space is given; the creator explores it systematically.
  3. **Transformational:** Transforming the conceptual space itself — altering the rules that define what is possible within the space. Example: Schoenberg abandoning tonality, Picasso inventing cubism. This is the rarest and most profound type — it changes what can be thought, not just what is thought within existing boundaries.
- **Conceptual spaces.** A "conceptual space" is a structured set of possibilities defined by enabling constraints (rules, conventions, physical laws). Exploratory creativity maps this space. Transformational creativity alters the constraints, creating a new space.
- **P-creativity vs. H-creativity.** Boden distinguishes psychological creativity (P-creativity: novel to the individual) from historical creativity (H-creativity: novel to the entire culture). The cognitive mechanisms are the same; the difference is the scope of the audience.
- **Computational creativity is possible in principle.** Boden argues that if creativity can be scientifically described in terms of combinational, exploratory, and transformational operations on conceptual spaces, then it can in principle be implemented in computational systems. This is an argument about possibility, not a claim that current AI systems are creative.

## Methodology

Theoretical analysis combining cognitive science, artificial intelligence, and historical case studies. Boden analyzes examples of creativity in art, science, and music through the lens of her taxonomy, and reviews AI systems that implement aspects of each type (AARON for visual art, EMI for music composition, AM for mathematical discovery). No new empirical experiments are presented.

## Key Quotes

> "Transformational creativity is fundamentally different from combinational and exploratory creativity. It does not rearrange elements within a conceptual space or explore the boundaries of a space. It transforms the space itself — altering the rules that define what is and is not possible."

> "Explaining creativity does not explain it away. Understanding the mechanisms by which a sunset's colors are produced does not make the sunset less beautiful."

> "A merely novel idea is not necessarily a creative idea. Creative ideas are novel, surprising, AND valuable. The 'and valuable' is what distinguishes creativity from mere randomness."

## Limitations

Boden's framework is primarily conceptual rather than empirical — the taxonomy is proposed on theoretical grounds and illustrated with examples, but the three types are not empirically distinguished by measurement. The boundary between exploratory and transformational creativity is not always clear — is a radical exploration of a space the same as a transformation of it? The computational creativity argument has been overtaken by developments in AI (particularly large language models) that raise questions Boden's 1990/2004 framework does not address. The book also does not engage deeply with the neuroscience of creativity.
