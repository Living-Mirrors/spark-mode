# The Emerging Goddess: The Creative Process in Art, Science, and Other Fields

**Author:** Albert Rothenberg, Department of Psychiatry, Yale University School of Medicine
**Published:** University of Chicago Press, 1979. ISBN: 978-0-226-72964-7
**DOI/Link:** https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/E/bo3625177.html
**Field:** Psychiatry, Creativity Research, Psychodynamic Theory

---

## Abstract

*The Emerging Goddess* is Rothenberg's comprehensive theory of creative cognition, extending the Janusian thinking concept introduced in his 1971 paper and adding a second creative cognitive operation: "homospatial thinking" — the active conception of two or more discrete entities occupying the same space. The book draws on over a decade of clinical research with Nobel laureates, major writers, artists, and scientists to argue that creative thought involves specific, identifiable cognitive operations that can be distinguished from everyday thinking, psychotic thinking, and dreaming. The title refers to Rothenberg's metaphor for the creative process: something new emerging into consciousness, as Aphrodite emerged from the sea.

## Key Findings

- **Expanded Janusian thinking.** The full clinical and experimental evidence for Janusian thinking is presented, including: (a) structured interviews with Nobel laureates in physics, chemistry, and medicine (N = 22 over the course of the research program); (b) process studies of writers composing in real time (manuscripts with revisions analyzed); (c) controlled experiments comparing word association patterns of creative and non-creative populations.
- **Homospatial thinking.** A second creative operation: "actively conceiving two or more discrete entities occupying the same space, a conception leading to the articulation of new identities." While Janusian thinking holds opposites simultaneously, homospatial thinking superimposes entities — like seeing two transparent images overlaid, producing a new composite.
- **Distinction from psychotic thinking.** Creative thinking and psychotic thinking both involve unusual combinations of ideas, but they are structurally different. Psychotic thinking conflates categories (loosening of associations). Creative thinking deliberately juxtaposes categories while maintaining their distinctness. The creative thinker knows the two frames are incompatible; the psychotic thinker does not.
- **Resolution destroys the creative resource.** The book's most important finding for Spark Mode: when creators prematurely resolve a Janusian opposition — choosing one pole over the other, or synthesizing into a compromise — the creative process terminates. The sustained tension between opposites IS the generative force. Collapse the tension and you collapse the creativity.

## Methodology

Longitudinal clinical research combining: (1) Semi-structured interviews with Nobel laureates (N = 22, conducted over 10+ years); (2) Real-time process studies of writers composing (Rothenberg sat with writers during composition sessions and analyzed their manuscripts, including deletions and revisions, N = 8); (3) Controlled word association experiments (expanded from the 1971 study, total N exceeding 100); (4) Historical case analyses of documented creative breakthroughs in science and art.

## Key Quotes

> "When the creator prematurely resolves the opposition — choosing one pole, compromising, or synthesizing — the creative process dies. The tension between the poles is not a problem to be solved. It is the engine of creation."

> "Creative thinking is not dreaming, not free association, not psychotic loosening. It is the most purposeful and structured form of thinking, operating with specific cognitive tools — Janusian and homospatial thinking — that are identifiable, measurable, and distinct."

> "The creator holds two faces looking in opposite directions and refuses to choose between them. From that refusal, something new emerges."

## Limitations

The longitudinal research program, while impressively sustained, relies on a relatively small number of participants at the highest creative level (22 Nobel laureates). The real-time process studies of writers (N = 8) provide rich data but cannot be considered representative. Rothenberg's psychodynamic framework, while producing the Janusian thinking concept, also includes theoretical commitments (about the relationship between creativity and psychopathology, about the role of primary process thinking) that have been contested. The homospatial thinking concept has been less influential and less empirically validated than Janusian thinking.
