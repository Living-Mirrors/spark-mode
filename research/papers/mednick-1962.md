# The Associative Basis of the Creative Process

**Author:** Sarnoff A. Mednick, Department of Psychology, University of Michigan
**Published:** *Psychological Review*, 69(3), 220–232, 1962
**DOI/Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/h0048850
**Field:** Cognitive Psychology, Creativity Research

---

## Abstract

This paper proposes that creative thinking is fundamentally an associative process and that individual differences in creativity can be understood in terms of the *shape* of associative hierarchies. Individuals with "steep" hierarchies produce a few strong associates to any given stimulus, with activation dropping off rapidly. Individuals with "flat" hierarchies maintain activation across a much broader range of associates, including semantically remote ones. Creative thinking, on this model, is the formation of associative elements into new combinations that meet specified requirements — and the probability of creative solutions increases as the associative hierarchy flattens. The paper introduces the Remote Associates Test (RAT) as an operational measure of this capacity.

## Key Findings

- **Steep vs. flat associative hierarchies.** The key dimension distinguishing creative from non-creative thinking is not the quality of associations but their *distribution.* A steep hierarchy concentrates activation on dominant associates ("table" → "chair"); a flat hierarchy maintains activation across remote associates ("table" → "negotiation," "periodic," "water table").
- **Three mechanisms for creative association.** (a) Serendipity — contiguity in experience brings remote elements into proximity. (b) Similarity — elements share an abstract property not previously recognized. (c) Mediation — a chain of associations connects two remote elements through intermediaries.
- **The Remote Associates Test (RAT).** Given three words (e.g., "cottage," "Swiss," "cake"), find the word that connects all three ("cheese"). The RAT operationalizes the capacity to find remote associations across semantic distance. It has been validated across hundreds of subsequent studies as a reliable measure of creative potential, with correlations to real-world creative achievement ranging from r = .20 to r = .40.
- **Flat hierarchies are trainable.** While individuals differ in their baseline associative gradient, conditions can be manipulated to flatten the hierarchy: reduced anxiety, playful mood, low arousal, and exposure to diverse stimuli all broaden the associative search.

## Methodology

Theoretical paper with psychometric instrument development. Mednick develops the theoretical framework from associationist psychology (extending Hull's habit-family hierarchy concept) and introduces the RAT as an operational measure. Initial validation data are presented comparing RAT scores to independently rated creative achievement in architecture students (N = 43, data from IPAR studies at UC Berkeley).

## Relevance to Spark Mode

**Principle:** #3 — Remote Reaching
**Specific connection:** Mednick's flat associative hierarchy is the direct theoretical basis for Principle #3. Remote Reaching actively deploys the flat hierarchy — resisting the pull of dominant associates, maintaining activation across semantic distance, deliberately reaching past the obvious. The principle is not about "thinking harder" but about broadening the activation gradient to let remote associations stay live.
**What this paper provides that others don't:** The foundational model of creative association. Everything in the specification about associative reach — resisting the obvious, maintaining activation across distance, reaching into foreign domains — traces back to this 1962 paper. Martindale (1995) provided the neural mechanism, but Mednick provided the model.

## Key Quotes

> "The creative thinking process is the forming of associative elements into new combinations which either meet specified requirements or are in some way useful. The more mutually remote the elements of the new combination, the more creative the process or solution."

> "The greater the number of associations that an individual has to the requisite elements of a problem, the greater the probability of his reaching a creative solution."

> "The individual differences in creative potential can be understood as differences in associative hierarchy: the creative person has a flat hierarchy extending to remote associates; the uncreative person has a steep hierarchy concentrated on dominant associates."

## Limitations

The RAT measures convergent remote association (finding a single correct answer), not divergent creative production (generating many novel ideas). Critics (e.g., Worthen & Clark, 1971) have argued that the RAT is better described as a measure of verbal fluency than creativity per se. The associative model does not account for the evaluative component of creativity — how remote combinations are assessed for value. The model also does not address what happens after remote elements are activated — how they combine into something new. Subsequent research (Koestler, 1964; Ward et al., 1995) addressed this gap.
