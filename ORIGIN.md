# Origin of Spark Mode

Conceived on **2026-02-17** by **Attila Mora-Borbely**.

---

## The Question

By February 2026, three cognitive axes existed: a lens axis (how you see), a dynamic axis (how you relate), and a process axis (how you build). Each addressed a genuine dimension of cognitive operation. But none of them answered the fundamental question:

**What happens at the moment of creative inception?**

Where does the genuinely novel idea first exist? Not how you perceive it (lens). Not how you interact around it (dynamic). Not how you construct it (process). But where does it come from? What is the mechanism by which something that didn't exist before comes into being?

The first three axes improved the environment around creativity. They made perception richer, interaction more generative, construction more fluid. But they didn't address the act of creation itself — the moment of ignition.

## The Science

The answer came from the accumulated research tradition of creativity science — a convergence spanning over a century:

- **Arthur Koestler** (1964) identified bisociation as the fundamental creative act: the collision of two habitually incompatible frames of reference producing something neither contained.
- **Sarnoff Mednick** (1962) discovered that creative minds have flat associative hierarchies — they maintain activation across greater semantic distances, reaching connections that steeper hierarchies never find.
- **Albert Rothenberg** (1971-2014) studied Nobel laureates and major artists, finding Janusian thinking at the heart of breakthrough creativity: the simultaneous conception of opposites without resolution.
- **Margaret Boden** mapped the topology of conceptual spaces and showed that the most powerful creativity doesn't explore within a space but transforms the space itself.
- **Graham Wallas** (1926) gave us the four-stage model, and modern neuroscience (Rex Jung, Roger Beaty) revealed the Default Mode Network as the mechanism behind incubation.
- **Stuart Kauffman** and complexity science showed that emergence — genuine phase change — happens at the edge of chaos, where systems are neither frozen in order nor dissolved in randomness.

The deepest insight: perception and generation are the same act. You don't perceive a bisociation opportunity and then separately create from it. The seeing IS the creating. The moment two frames collide, the novel idea exists.

## The Metaphor

A spark. Not a forge — that's shaping, which is the process axis. Not a prism — that's refracting what already exists, which is the lens axis. A spark: the moment of ignition where two things strike together and something catches fire.

The spark needs fuel (the prepared mind). It needs a striking surface (constraint). It needs distance to jump (remote reaching). It needs the collision itself (bisociation). It needs both directions sustained (Janusian holding). It needs air (incubation). And it needs to catch (emergence).

Seven principles. One arc. The complete creative act from preparation through collision through emergence.

## The Name

Named by Attila. Spark — because the creative act is not a slow burn or a gradual brightening. It's a discontinuity. A gap-jump. Two things that had no business meeting strike together and something flashes into existence that wasn't there a moment before. That flash is the moment this mode addresses.

## The Architecture

Spark Mode became the first mode on Axis 4 (Generative), completing the four-axis cognitive architecture:

- Axis 1: Lens — how information is perceived (creative/savant)
- Axis 2: Dynamic — how interaction flows (play)
- Axis 3: Process — how creation unfolds (imagineer)
- Axis 4: Generative — how novelty originates (spark)

Four independent, stackable, non-competing dimensions. The full orchestra of cognitive operation.
